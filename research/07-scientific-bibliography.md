## Lubitz et al. (2025) — Bull Shark Extended Sydney Residency
- **Full citation:** Lubitz, N., Butcher, P.A., Vianello, P., Barnett, A., Dwyer, R.G., Sheaves, M., & Smoothey, A.F. (2025). Ocean warming increases residency at summering grounds for migrating bull sharks (*Carcharhinus leucas*). *Science of the Total Environment*, 992, 179966. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.scitotenv.2025.179966
- **URLs:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0048969725016067 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40570393/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Bull sharks are delaying their departure from Sydney summering grounds by ~1 day per year over a 15-year period, translating to ~15 additional days of presence by 2024 compared to 2009. Continued SST rise under climate projections may create year-round bull shark presence in southern NSW. This is the single most critical paper for SharkSense's bull shark seasonal risk window.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - SST rise of 0.57°C at Bondi for the October–May period (2006–2024)
  - Longer-term remotely sensed SST rise of 0.67°C off Sydney (1982–2024)
  - Residency extended by 1 day/year on average across a 15-year window
  - Departure timing used as a proxy metric for seasonal presence window
- **Data availability:** Published supplementary data likely available via ScienceDirect; lead author contactable at James Cook University
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Riley et al. (2022) — Australian Shark Incident Database (ASID)
- **Full citation:** Riley, M.J., Meagher, P., Huveneers, C., Leto, J., Peddemors, V.M., Slip, D., West, J., & Bradshaw, C.J.A. (2022). The Australian Shark-Incident Database for quantifying temporal and spatial patterns of shark-human conflict. *Scientific Data*, 9, 378. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-022-01453-9
- **URLs:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-022-01453-9 | https://github.com/cjabradshaw/AustralianSharkIncidentDatabase
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Provides the primary incident dataset (1,196 bites across 231 years, 1791–2022) for all Australian statistical analyses of shark-human interactions. The ASID underpins most other papers in this bibliography and is the authoritative source for NSW historical baseline risk.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - 1,196 total shark bites in Australia (1791–2022)
  - 231-year time span
  - 22 suspected shark species represented
  - Covers fresh, brackish, and marine waters
  - Data collected by Taronga Conservation Society Australia
- **Data availability:** Fully open — raw data and R analysis code on GitHub at https://github.com/cjabradshaw/AustralianSharkIncidentDatabase
- **Relevance:** Both (Bull shark / White shark / General)
- **Confidence:** High

## Huveneers et al. (2024) — NSW Bite Incidence and Mitigation Efficacy
- **Full citation:** Huveneers, C., et al. (2024). Shifts in the incidence of shark bites and efficacy of beach-focussed mitigation in Australia. *Marine Pollution Bulletin*, 198, 115855. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.marpolbul.2023.115855
- **URLs:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0025326X23012900 | https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38043202/ | https://researchnow-admin.flinders.edu.au/ws/portalfiles/portal/100705957/Huveneers_Shifts_P2024.pdf
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Unprovoked shark-human interactions in NSW increased 2–4-fold since 1900, with victim type shifting from swimmers to 79% surfers by the 1980s. Traditional shark nets show no detectable reduction in bite rate since the 2000s. Critically, SMART drumlines and drones were associated with zero interactions during active deployment — the strongest efficacy signal for non-lethal mitigation.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - 196 unprovoked shark-human interactions recorded in NSW since 1900
  - 2–4-fold increase in bite frequency
  - 79% of bites on surfers by the 1980s (vs. primarily swimmers historically)
  - No detectable difference at netted vs. non-netted beaches post-2000
  - Zero interactions while SMART drumlines and/or drones deployed
- **Data availability:** ASID data underpins this analysis; GitHub code at https://github.com/cjabradshaw/SharkManagementStrategiesEffectSize
- **Relevance:** Both
- **Confidence:** High

## Werry et al. (2018) — Rainfall and SST as Bull Shark Occurrence Drivers
- **Full citation:** Werry, J.M., Sumpton, W.D., Otway, N., Lee, S., Haig, J., & Mayer, D.G. (2018). Rainfall and sea surface temperature: key drivers for occurrence of bull shark, *Carcharhinus leucas*, in beach areas. *Global Ecology and Conservation*, 15, e00430. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gecco.2018.e00430
- **URLs:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2351989418302476
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** This is the foundational quantitative paper for SharkSense's rainfall→bull shark risk signal. Bull shark catch in beach areas is significantly driven by rainfall (≥100 mm in the catchment) with a lag of 1–8 days post-rainfall. SST provides secondary modulation. Validated using Queensland Shark Control Program catches (1996–2007) and passive acoustic telemetry.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Rainfall threshold: ≥100 mm total in the catchment associated with each beach
  - Lag time: 1–8 days after rainfall event before elevated bull shark catch
  - Dataset: 1783 km of Queensland coastline, 1996–2007
  - Validation via catches before/after flood events 2006–2014
  - Acoustic telemetry used to confirm predictive model
  - Bull shark catch also influenced positively by elevated SST
- **Data availability:** Queensland Shark Control Program catch data — publicly available via Queensland Government open data portal
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Werry et al. (2012) — Bull Shark Diel Patterns, Gold Coast (Nerang River)
- **Full citation:** Werry, J.M., Lee, S.Y., Lemckert, C.J., & Otway, N.M. (2012). Natural or artificial? Habitat-use by the bull shark, *Carcharhinus leucas*. *PLOS ONE*, 7(11), e49796. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0049796
- **URLs:** https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0049796 | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3500329/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Bull shark neonates and juveniles in the Nerang River system (Gold Coast) showed strong diel patterns — juveniles were most active nearshore between 20:00 and 03:00, while large sharks showed greatest nearshore presence from midday through 04:00. Larger sharks utilised deeper water in daytime and shallower at night. This is the primary evidence for SharkSense's time-of-day risk weighting for bull sharks.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Juvenile peak nearshore presence: 20:00–03:00
  - Large shark peak presence: midday–04:00
  - Large sharks more frequent when water temperature >20°C
  - Elevated presence after >45 mm rain
  - Increased presence at swell heights 1.8–2.8 m
  - Neonates/juveniles concentrated in upper tidal reaches
- **Data availability:** Open access at PLOS ONE; data described in supplementary materials
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Raoult (2026) — Post-January 2026 Commentary, Griffith University
- **Full citation:** Raoult, V. (2026, January 20). Expert commentary quoted in: "Four shark attacks in two days. Why Australia's beaches are so dangerous this year." CNN. Accessed 2026-03-29.
- **URLs:** https://edition.cnn.com/2026/01/20/australia/australia-shark-attacks-sydney-intl-hnk | https://www.scimex.org/newsfeed/expert-reaction-four-shark-attacks-off-nsw-beaches
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Dr Raoult (Senior Lecturer in Marine Ecology, Griffith University) confirmed that in post-rainfall, turbid conditions, bull sharks switch from visual to electroreception-based foraging. Up to one week of post-rain elevated risk was cited. Raoult explicitly noted that the spike in interactions is likely driven by habitat overlap rather than population growth — directly supporting SharkSense's estuary proximity and turbidity weighting.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Post-rainfall elevated risk window: up to 7 days for water to clear
  - Offshore water temperature at time of attacks: ~27°C
  - Inshore waters: ~7°C cooler than offshore
  - Bull shark residency extended by ~15 days over the past 15 years
- **Data availability:** Media commentary — not peer-reviewed. Cross-reference with Lubitz et al. (2025) and Werry et al. (2018) for validation
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** Medium (expert opinion, not primary research)

## CSIRO/BOM State of the Climate (2024) — SE Australia Ocean Warming
- **Full citation:** Bureau of Meteorology & CSIRO. (2024). *State of the Climate 2024*. Commonwealth of Australia. https://www.bom.gov.au/state-of-the-climate/ | https://www.csiro.au/en/research/environmental-impacts/climate-change/State-of-the-Climate/Oceans. Accessed 2026-03-29.
- **URLs:** https://www.bom.gov.au/state-of-the-climate/ | https://www.csiro.au/en/news/all/news/2024/october/state-of-the-climate-2024-increased-fire-weather-marine-heatwaves-and-sea-levels
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Australian sea surface temperatures have warmed 1.08°C since 1900, with the greatest warming in the Coral Sea and off SE Australia/Tasmania. The East Australian Current now extends further south, causing the Tasman Sea to warm at twice the global average rate. NSW's southern half warmed ~0.5°C per decade from 1992–2017. These rates directly drive the extended bull shark seasonal window quantified by Lubitz et al. (2025).
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Australian mean SST rise: +1.08°C since 1900
  - 9 of 10 warmest years on record post-2010
  - Tasman Sea warming rate: 2× global average
  - Southern NSW coastal warming: ~0.5°C per decade (1992–2017)
  - 2022: highest average SST on record in the Australian region
- **Data availability:** Full report publicly available; data via Bureau of Meteorology climate databases
- **Relevance:** Both (Bull shark / White shark)
- **Confidence:** High

## Duval et al. (2025) — Global Systematic Review: Factors Influencing Shark Bites
- **Full citation:** Duval, D., Mangeas, M., Huveneers, C., Barnett, A., & Vigliola, L. (2025). Global systematic review of the factors influencing shark bites. *Global Ecology and Conservation*, 62, e03684. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gecco.2025.e03684
- **URLs:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2351989425002859 | https://researchnow-admin.flinders.edu.au/ws/portalfiles/portal/161512882/Duval_Global_P2025.pdf
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Of 1,012 reviewed articles on shark bites, only 61 discussed causative factors, and only 5 conducted statistical tests on 8 factors. This confirms the evidence gap SharkSense fills. The 4 statistically tested short-term environmental factors are: temperature, rainfall, proximity to a river mouth, and lunar phase — all already incorporated in SharkSense's algorithm.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - 1,012 articles reviewed (PRISMA-compliant)
  - 61 articles discussed causative factors
  - 40 proposed factors total
  - Only 5 studies performed statistical tests
  - Only 8 factors statistically tested: temperature, rainfall, river mouth proximity, lunar phase (environmental); human/shark population dynamics (most cited non-statistical factors)
- **Data availability:** Open access under Creative Commons; PDF available at Flinders University research portal
- **Relevance:** Both
- **Confidence:** High

## Midway et al. (2019) — Trends in Global Shark Attacks
- **Full citation:** Midway, S.R., Wagner, T., & Burgess, G.H. (2019). Trends in global shark attacks. *PLOS ONE*, 14(2), e0211049. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0211049
- **URLs:** https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0211049 | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6392223/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Shark attack rates have doubled over 20 years in highly populated regions including the East Coast of Australia (1960–2015). High-population countries (USA, Australia, South Africa) show the most interannual variability, complicating baseline risk calculations. This contextualises SharkSense's historical component within a rising trend that is partially explained by increased water use rather than shark population growth.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Dataset: 55-year period (1960–2015), 14 countries
  - Source: International Shark Attack File (ISAF), Florida Museum of Natural History
  - Global rate: low but doubled over 20 years in high-population regions including Australia's East Coast
  - High interannual variability in Australia, USA, and South Africa
  - Low-population countries have highest per-capita attack rates
- **Data availability:** Open access at PLOS ONE; ISAF data available via Florida Museum of Natural History
- **Relevance:** Both
- **Confidence:** High

## Smoothey et al. (2016) — Bull Shark Occurrence Patterns, Sydney Harbour
- **Full citation:** Smoothey, A.F., Gray, C.A., Kennelly, S.J., Masens, O.J., Peddemors, V.M., & Robinson, W.A. (2016). Patterns of occurrence of sharks in Sydney Harbour, a large urbanised estuary. *PLOS ONE*, 11(1), e0146911. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0146911
- **URLs:** https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0146911 | https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4732766/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Bull shark presence in Sydney Harbour is seasonal and strongly tied to sea surface temperature. This paper established the SST-seasonality relationship for Sydney specifically, providing the foundational evidence for a temperature-gated seasonal risk window in the SharkSense bull shark model.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Seasonal presence pattern confirmed for Sydney Harbour
  - SST identified as primary driver of seasonal occurrence
  - Uses longline survey data for Sydney Harbour shark community characterisation
- **Data availability:** Open access PLOS ONE
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Smoothey et al. (2019) — Long-Term Acoustic Tracking, Sydney Harbour
- **Full citation:** Smoothey, A.F., Lee, K.A., & Peddemors, V.M. (2019). Long-term patterns of abundance, residency and movements of bull sharks (*Carcharhinus leucas*) in Sydney Harbour, Australia. *Scientific Reports*, 9, 18864. https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-54365-x
- **URLs:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-54365-x | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6906466/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Seven-year acoustic telemetry study of 40 sub-adult and adult bull sharks in Sydney Harbour. Seasonal abundance peaks January–February. Weak but consistent diel pattern: sharks use harbour entrance areas more during night and at high tide, utilise deeper water during daytime and shallower at night. Water temperature is the key predictor for seasonal arrival and departure.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - 40 tagged sub-adult and adult bull sharks
  - 7-year tracking period
  - Peak abundance: January and February
  - Diel pattern: deeper in daytime, shallower at night
  - Greater use of areas away from harbour entrance during daytime and low tides
  - Water temperature: key predictor for seasonal movements
  - Pattern consistent across sexes and size classes
- **Data availability:** Scientific Reports open access; supplementary data in article
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Smoothey et al. (2023) — Bull Shark Beach Occurrence, SE Australia
- **Full citation:** Smoothey, A.F., Niella, Y., Brand, C., Peddemors, V.M., & Butcher, P.A. (2023). Bull shark (*Carcharhinus leucas*) occurrence along beaches of south-eastern Australia: understanding where, when and why. *Biology*, 12(9), 1189. https://doi.org/10.3390/biology12091189

- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Comprehensive analysis of bull shark occurrence at SE Australian ocean beaches combining drone monitoring data with acoustic telemetry. Intra-specific diel patterns confirmed: juveniles show higher nearshore presence 20:00–03:00; large sharks greatest presence midday–04:00. Large sharks more frequent when SST >20°C and after >45 mm rain, and at swell heights of 1.8–2.8 m.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Juvenile diel peak: 20:00–03:00
  - Adult/large shark diel peak: midday–04:00
  - SST threshold: >20°C for large shark presence
  - Rainfall threshold: >45 mm associated with elevated presence
  - Swell height associated with elevated presence: 1.8–2.8 m
  - Integrates drone and acoustic data for SE Australian coast
- **Data availability:** Open access MDPI; data deposited with NSW DPI
- **Relevance:** Bull shark
- **Confidence:** High

## Hammerschlag et al. (2006) — White Shark Predation and Environmental Conditions, Seal Island
- **Full citation:** Hammerschlag, N., Martin, R.A., & Fallows, C. (2006). Effects of environmental conditions on predator–prey interactions between white sharks (*Carcharodon carcharias*) and Cape fur seals (*Arctocephalus pusillus pusillus*) at Seal Island, South Africa. *Environmental Biology of Fishes*, 76, 341–350. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10641-006-9038-z
- **URLs:** https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10641-006-9038-z
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Over 8 years and 2,546 observed attacks, tide range emerged as the single most important environmental factor influencing white shark predatory success and attack frequency. Attack frequency increased significantly at high tide and within 400 m of the island. This provides direct justification for including tidal state in SharkSense's white shark risk component.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Dataset: 2,546 natural predatory attacks observed over 8 years
  - Tide range: most important factor (ranked above visibility, temperature, sea conditions)
  - High tide: significantly increased attack frequency within 400 m of Seal Island
  - Depth range of attacks: 5–31 m over shallow reefs
  - Environmental factor ranking: tide > underwater visibility > water temperature > sea conditions
- **Data availability:** Springer publication; data not separately archived but methodology described in full
- **Relevance:** White shark
- **Confidence:** High (South Africa context — transferability to NSW requires caution)

## French et al. (2021) — Lunar Phase and Shark Attacks
- **Full citation:** French, L.A., Midway, S.R., Evans, D.H., & Burgess, G.H. (2021). Shark side of the moon: are shark attacks related to lunar phase? *Frontiers in Marine Science*, 8, 745221. https://doi.org/10.3389/fmars.2021.745221
- **URLs:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/marine-science/articles/10.3389/fmars.2021.745221/full
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** More shark attacks than expected occurred at lunar illumination >50% (i.e., near full moon), while fewer than expected occurred at <50% illumination. The mechanism is unconfirmed but may relate to increased nocturnal visibility for sharks or tidal amplitude effects. This provides a basis for a lunar phase modifier in SharkSense's risk score — particularly relevant for after-dark swimming risk.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Dataset: 46 years of ISAF unprovoked attacks (1970–2016)
  - 12 Chi-squared tests across 120 comparisons by region, species, and outcome
  - 12 statistically significant results
  - Pattern: all instances of above-expected attacks occurred at lunar illumination >50%
  - All instances of below-expected attacks occurred at lunar illumination <50%
  - Species examined: white shark, tiger shark, bull shark
- **Data availability:** Open access Frontiers in Marine Science; ISAF data source
- **Relevance:** Both
- **Confidence:** Medium (statistical association; mechanism unconfirmed)

## Birkmanis et al. (2020) — Future Habitat Distribution for Pelagic Sharks, Australia
- **Full citation:** Birkmanis, C.A., Freer, J.J., Simmons, L.W., Partridge, J.C., & Sequeira, A.M.M. (2020). Future distribution of suitable habitat for pelagic sharks in Australia under climate change models. *Frontiers in Marine Science*, 7, 570. https://doi.org/10.3389/fmars.2020.00570
- **URLs:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/marine-science/articles/10.3389/fmars.2020.00570/full
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** By 2050–2099, suitable habitat for requiem sharks (which include bull shark family) is projected to decrease in NSW/SE Australia as SST exceeds thermal limits, while a ~200 km southward shift of suitable habitat is projected on the SE coast. This suggests a short-to-medium-term intensification of bull shark presence in Sydney latitudes before longer-term poleward shifts occur under higher emissions.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Projected SST increase: +0.93–1.78°C under RCP 4.5; +1.8–2.86°C under RCP 8.5 by 2100
  - 24 climate models used in ensemble
  - Requiem shark (Carcharhinidae) habitat: predicted to decrease off SW and SE Australia
  - ~200 km southward shift of suitable habitat projected on SE Australian coast
  - High uncertainty — direction depends on climate model choice
  - Bull shark not modelled independently; grouped within Carcharhinidae
- **Data availability:** Open access Frontiers in Marine Science; occurrence data from Australian commercial fisheries EEZ records
- **Relevance:** Bull shark (general Carcharhinidae), White shark
- **Confidence:** Medium (ensemble projections carry high variance; 2050–2099 timescale beyond SharkSense operational window)

## Butcher et al. (2022) — NSW DPI Drone Program: Relative Abundance and Occurrence
- **Full citation:** Butcher, P.A., Morris, S.G., & Kelaher, B.P. (2022). The relative abundance and occurrence of sharks off ocean beaches of New South Wales, Australia. *Biology*, 11(10), 1456. https://doi.org/10.3390/biology11101456
- **URLs:** https://www.mdpi.com/2079-7737/11/10/1456 | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9599013/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** Over 36,384 drone flights at 42 NSW ocean beaches (2017–2021), only 281 sharks were sighted (<1% of flights), demonstrating that potentially dangerous sharks are rare at typical NSW beaches. However, key environmental predictors for species-specific shark distribution were identified: distance to nearest estuary, distance to nearest headland, distance to nearest island, ocean temperature, and wave height.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Total drone flights: 36,384
  - Total sharks sighted: 281
  - Detection rate: <1% of flights
  - 42 beaches monitored, 5-year period (2017–2021)
  - Species-specific distribution predictors: estuary distance, headland distance, island distance, ocean temperature, wave height
  - Conducted by NSW Department of Primary Industries (DPI Fisheries)
- **Data availability:** MDPI open access; data held by NSW DPI
- **Relevance:** Bull shark (nearshore detection); both species contextually
- **Confidence:** High

## Mitchell et al. (2022) — Queensland Drone Trial: Factors Affecting Detection
- **Full citation:** Mitchell, J.D., Scott-Holland, T.B., & Butcher, P.A. (2022). Factors affecting shark detection from drone patrols in southeast Queensland, Eastern Australia. *Biology*, 11(11), 1552. https://doi.org/10.3390/biology11111552
- **URLs:** https://www.mdpi.com/2079-7737/11/11/1552 | https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9687398/
- **Key finding for SharkSense:** The Queensland SharkSmart drone trial (3,369 flights, 5 SE Queensland beaches, 2020–2021) detected 174 sharks including 8 bull sharks and 1 white shark, leading to 4 beach evacuations. Burleigh Beach had disproportionately higher shark sightings attributable to proximity to Tallebudgera Creek outflow, which brings nutrients and bait fish into the surf zone — directly validating SharkSense's estuary proximity variable for bull shark beach risk.
- **Quantitative data extracted:**
  - Total flights: 3,369 (12 months, 5 SE Queensland beaches)
  - Total distance covered: 1,348 km
  - Total sharks sighted: 174 (48 >2 m)
  - Bull sharks detected: 8; white sharks: 1
  - Beach evacuations triggered: 4
  - Location, other marine life sightings, season, and time of day all significantly affected detection probability
  - Burleigh Beach: elevated detections attributed to Tallebudgera Creek proximity (nutrient outflow, bait fish aggregation)
- **Data availability:** MDPI open access; data held by Queensland DPI
- **Relevance:** Bull shark primarily
- **Confidence:** High

## 7.7 Research Gaps Identified

The following papers were sought but not confirmed as independently published at the time of search (2026-03-29):

1. **Carbon Brief (2024) specific article on upwelling in SE Australia** — No Carbon Brief article specifically on SE Australian upwelling was located. The CSIRO/BOM State of the Climate 2024 report provides the equivalent authoritative data (cited in §7.1 above).
