## 6.1 NSW Shark Management Program — Current State

### Infrastructure Overview

The NSW Shark Management Program, operated by NSW Department of Primary Industries and Regional Development (DPIRD) under the SharkSmart brand, is described as the world's largest shark management program. As of the 2025/26 season, the full infrastructure suite is:

| Infrastructure | Count | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| SMART drumlines | 305 | 19 LGAs, Tweed Heads to Pambula, year-round |
| Shark nets | 51 beaches | 8 LGAs, Newcastle to Wollongong, Sept–March only |
| Tagged shark listening stations | 37 | Statewide, at least 1 per LGA, year-round |
| Drone patrol locations (SLS NSW) | 50 base + 30 added Jan 2026 | Tweed to Bega |
| Surfing NSW boardrider club drones | 60+ clubs | Newcastle to Wollongong |

**Listening stations:** The 37 VR4G acoustic receivers detect acoustically tagged white sharks, tiger sharks, and bull sharks that swim within 500 m. Twenty of these stations are integrated with the IMOS national array. Detections are pushed to the SharkSmart app and @NSWSharkSmart Twitter/X in real time via satellite uplink.

**SMART drumlines:** Proven 15x more efficient than shark nets at catching target sharks, and catch significantly fewer non-target animals. All sharks caught are tagged, measured, and released alive.

**Drone operations 2025/26 to date (as of Jan 2026):** 29,431 flights, 8,044 flying hours, 461 sharks sighted, 170 countermeasures (evacuations, sirens, rescue vessels).

### Budget

| Funding component | Amount |
|---|---|
| Annual base program budget | $21.4 million |
| 2025/26 summer enhancement (Dec 2025) | $2.5 million |
| 2025/26 further expansion (Jan 2026) | $4.2 million |
| **Total 2025/26 investment** | **~$28.1 million** |
| Two-year cumulative (2024/25 + 2025/26) | $42+ million |

The $4.2 million January 2026 package represents a 90% increase in drone surveillance hours and funds the targeted bull shark tagging and monitoring program in Sydney Harbour.

The $6.7 million figure cited in the brief may aggregate the $2.5M (December) and $4.2M (January) enhancements ($6.7M total), which is accurate.

### 2026 Planned Changes

**Shark net phase-out (accelerated):** The NSW Government plans to end the shark net season one month early in 2026, replacing net coverage with expanded drone and surveillance operations. This follows:
- All eight affected councils voting against continued use of nets (the final vote was Randwick Council, February 25, 2025)
- A DPI-initiated trial removal of nets from nominated beaches for 2025/26
- Scientific findings that 60%+ of animals caught in nets in 2022/23 were non-target species

**Drone expansion:** 30 additional SLS NSW drone locations activated from January 24, 2026, including 19 new Sydney beaches and 11 regional sites. Extended operations through to end of April school holidays, seven days a week.

**Surfing NSW expansion:** $650,000 allocated for drone rollout to boardrider clubs and surf schools between Newcastle and Wollongong, adding a second parallel surveillance network to SLS NSW.

**March 2026 parliamentary inquiry proposal:** On 26 March 2026, during Question Time, the NSW Premier indicated support for a parliamentary committee inquiry into shark management — the first since 2016. This could trigger further policy changes.

### Randwick Council Decision

Randwick Council voted unanimously on February 25, 2025 against continuing shark nets at Maroubra and Coogee beaches. No target sharks had been caught at either beach since 2020; only non-target species including endangered sea turtles and critically endangered grey nurse sharks. The council is now trialling SMART drumlines and listening stations along the Randwick coastline as replacements.

**Implications for SharkSense:** The infrastructure transition toward listening stations and drone surveillance — and away from nets — increases the completeness and real-time nature of the data ecosystem SharkSense can draw upon. The expanded SLS NSW drone fleet means more frequent sighting data, and the new Sydney Harbour listening stations directly benefit bull shark detection for SharkSense's 5 pilot beaches.

**Key players:** NSW DPIRD (program owner), Surf Life Saving NSW (drone operator), Surfing NSW (expanded drone partner), local councils (approval authority for net deployment).

## 6.2 Bull Shark Tagging in Sydney Harbour

### The January 2026 Program

NSW DPIRD announced and commenced a focused bull shark tagging and monitoring program in Sydney Harbour and other estuaries as part of the January 2026 $4.2 million enhancement package. The program objectives are to:

1. Establish how many bull sharks reside in the harbour and key estuaries
2. Map their movement patterns, residency periods, and home ranges
3. Identify higher-risk periods, particularly after significant rainfall and flood events
4. Inform predictive risk modelling for 2026/27 and beyond

**Tagged in January 2026 (confirmed):**
- January 21, 2026 — 2.76 m bull shark tagged west of Harbour Bridge
- January 21, 2026 — 2.57 m bull shark tagged on the same night
- January 24, 2026 — 2.35 m bull shark tagged in the Parramatta River

**Historical context:** Since the tagging program began in 2009, 87 individual bull sharks have been tagged in Sydney Harbour (ranging 2.0–3.2 m total length, through to 2022). Each acoustic tag has a transmission life of approximately ten years.

### Listening Station Installation in Sydney Harbour

As part of the January 2026 expansion, NSW DPIRD is installing dedicated tagged shark listening stations within Sydney Harbour itself (separate from the existing coastal array). This is a significant development: previously, the 37 coastal listening stations did not comprehensively cover the harbour's internal waterways.

The harbour stations will provide real-time alerts via the SharkSmart app and @NSWSharkSmart when tagged bulls swim past a receiver — closing a major gap in the alert network for harbour users including kayakers, rowers, and swimmers at harbour beaches.

### Public Data Accessibility

**Current state (real-time alerts):** Detections from all listening stations — including the new harbour stations — are transmitted to a satellite and pushed to the public via the SharkSmart app and Twitter/X as they occur. This feed is public, though there is no documented open API.

**Historical and aggregated data:** Tag detection records are downloaded from listening stations periodically (typically in autumn). Some data flows through the IMOS national animal tracking array (20 of the 37 NSW stations are IMOS-connected), making historical detection records accessible via the IMOS/AODN Animal Tracking Database at https://animaltracking.aodn.org.au/.

**Restriction caveat:** IMOS notes that a minority of tag datasets have "Protected Species Status" with restricted access. Researchers must contact the point-of-contact (Rob Harcourt, Macquarie University, Robert.Harcourt@mq.edu.au) for access to restricted datasets.

**SharkSmart Pro:** A separate field mobility app (SharkSmart PRO) is used by DPIRD staff, SLS NSW, SMART drumline contractors, and shark meshing contractors. This appears to carry richer real-time operational data not exposed to the public.

### Data Access Implications for SharkSense

- Real-time alerts from the harbour stations will be publicly visible via the SharkSmart app/X feed — SharkSense can scrape @NSWSharkSmart for these alerts (already a planned scraper).
- The IMOS AODN portal provides historical detection datasets that could validate and train SharkSense's bull shark risk model. Access is generally open; contact IMOS for restricted datasets.
- There is no documented public REST API for real-time station detections. A partnership or data-sharing agreement with NSW DPIRD would be required for direct programmatic access to the detection feed.
- The research program explicitly aims to "develop a predictive tool for places and times of increased shark numbers" — SharkSense's goals align closely with DPIRD's own stated research agenda, creating a natural partnership angle.

## 6.3 Legal and Liability Considerations

### Regulatory Status: No Specific Prohibition on Publishing Risk Assessments

There is no Australian federal or NSW-specific regulation that prohibits a third party from publishing shark risk assessments or predictions. The legal landscape is governed by general frameworks:

**Relevant legislation:**
- *Australian Consumer Law (ACL)* (Schedule 2, Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth)) — prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct; requires that services be rendered with due care and skill. These guarantees cannot be excluded by disclaimer.
- *Fisheries Management Act 1994 (NSW)* — prohibits targeting, baiting, chasing, tagging, or marking threatened shark species. Publishing risk data does not engage this.
- *Biodiversity Conservation Act 2016 (NSW)* and *EPBC Act 1999 (Cth)* — protect great white sharks, grey nurse sharks, shortfin mako, school sharks, and porbeagle. No provision restricts publishing risk information.
- *Protection of the Environment Operations Act 1997 (NSW)* — governs sewage discharge (relevant to SharkSense's water quality data inputs, not to publishing outputs).

**Key conclusion:** Publishing a shark risk forecast is legally analogous to publishing weather or bushfire risk forecasts — it is lawful and unregulated, provided it is not misleading and carries appropriate disclaimers.

### Disclaimer Framework: Best Practice for Safety-Critical Apps in Australia

The following disclaimer patterns are used by comparable safety-critical apps operating in Australia:

**SharkSmart WA (Western Australia DPI):**
> "The information within the Application is designed to help you make an informed decision about your water use and take personal responsibility for your safety. Not all sightings are reported, and not all sharks are tagged or able to be detected. Changes in circumstances after the time of publication may impact the accuracy of that information."

**SafeWaters.ai (global shark risk forecast app):**
> "SafeWaters.AI does not predict shark attacks but provides risk forecasts based on historical and environmental data. The app disclaims liability for any harm or loss occurring despite the indicated risk level. Risk forecasts are not infallible; outliers and unanticipated attacks can occur. Always exercise your own judgment and take necessary precautions."

**Queensland Government (emergency information):**
> "The Queensland Government and Queensland Police Service will not be liable on any legal ground (including negligence) for direct, special, indirect, incidental, consequential, punitive, exemplary, or other losses arising out of the use of the material."

**South Australian emergency apps:**
> "We recommend that you do not rely on any one single source of information during an emergency."

**Core elements required for a SharkSense disclaimer:**

1. **Information only / not a guarantee:** Risk scores are forecasts based on available data, not guarantees of safety. Shark activity is unpredictable.
2. **Data completeness caveat:** Not all sharks are tagged. Not all sightings are reported. Sensor coverage is not total.
3. **Personal responsibility transfer:** Users must exercise their own judgment and take personal responsibility for decisions to enter the water.
4. **No liability clause:** SharkSense bears no liability for harm arising from use of or reliance on the app.
5. **Not a substitute:** The app is not a substitute for official beach patrol, lifeguard instructions, or government shark alerts.
6. **Placement requirement:** Under Australian Consumer Law guidance, the disclaimer must be "easy to find at the point a user is making a decision" — it should appear on the risk score display itself, not only in buried T&Cs.

**Limitation of disclaimer under ACL:** Disclaimers cannot exclude mandatory consumer guarantees, including the guarantee that a service is provided with due care and skill. SharkSense must ensure its underlying data and algorithm are not negligently constructed.

### Conservation Framing Requirements

While there are no legal mandates requiring a specific conservation framing, several contextual pressures make coexistence messaging strategically important:

- White sharks are listed as vulnerable under the EPBC Act and NSW Fisheries Management Act — any framing that encourages shark killing, hunting, or harassment would be unlawful.
- The NSW Shark Management Strategy explicitly frames its goal as coexistence, not eradication — government partners will expect aligned messaging from any app seeking data access.
- Over 50 organisations (marine scientists, conservationists, surfers, First Nations leaders) have backed the transition away from lethal control — public and media sentiment strongly favours non-lethal approaches.
- NSW DPI community research found that coexistence messaging and education were seen as "fundamental components of shark mitigation."

**SharkSense framing recommendation:** Risk levels should be framed as environmental conditions rather than threat levels (i.e., "conditions favour bull shark activity" rather than "danger: sharks present"). Include brief educational context (e.g., "Bull sharks use estuaries after rain — this is natural behaviour, not aggression") alongside risk scores. The SharkSense brand name and tone should reinforce informed coexistence, not fear.

## 6.4 Data Partnership Opportunities

### 6.4.1 NSW DPIRD Tagged Shark Detection Data

**Current accessibility (public):**
- Real-time detection alerts via SharkSmart app and @NSWSharkSmart (free, public)
- SharkSmart app appears to use an API backend at `api.sharksmart.hatchd.com.au` — no public documentation, appears unauthenticated but undocumented
- Historical SMART drumline catch data is published on the SharkSmart website as downloadable datasets
- IMOS-connected stations (20 of 37) feed into the AODN Animal Tracking Database — publicly accessible for most species, with a small minority under restricted access

**Formal partnership pathway:**
NSW DPIRD has an existing NSW Shark Management Strategy Annual Grants Program and a PhD/Postdoc Program for co-investment research. A formal data access agreement with DPIRD could yield:
- Direct API or database access to listening station detection events
- Access to SharkSmart PRO operational data
- Bull shark tagging metadata (size, sex, location, recapture history)

**Assessment:** Feasible, but requires formal application. The strongest pitch would frame SharkSense as complementary to DPIRD's own stated goal of "developing a predictive tool for places and times of increased shark numbers." The alignment is strong and recent (January 2026 announcement). Contact point: NSW DPIRD SharkSmart team via sharksmart.nsw.gov.au or the IMOS Animal Tracking Facility (Robert.Harcourt@mq.edu.au for acoustic data access).

**Risk:** DPIRD may regard a public-facing predictive risk app as competing with or undermining official government messaging. This can be mitigated by positioning SharkSense as an aggregator of government data with government-aligned messaging and clear attribution.

### 6.4.2 Surf Life Saving NSW Drone Sighting Data

**Current state:** SLS NSW operates 50+ drone patrol locations statewide under contract to NSW DPIRD. All shark sighting data from drone operations is reported into the SharkSmart system and published via the SharkSmart app. SLS NSW does not appear to operate a separate public data portal for drone sightings.

**Volume (2025/26 to Jan 2026):** 461 sharks sighted across 29,431 flights — a rich real-time dataset. Sightings include species (where identifiable), location, time, and countermeasure taken.

**Partnership feasibility:** SLS NSW is a government-contracted operator, not an independent data owner. Any data-sharing arrangement would likely require DPIRD's consent, since SLS NSW sighting data flows into the SharkSmart system under the government contract. Direct partnership with SLS NSW alone is unlikely to yield data access independent of DPIRD.

**However:** SLS NSW is actively seeking innovation partnerships — they are currently exploring long-range drones and AI detection systems. A technology partnership (providing SharkSense's risk forecasting in exchange for sighting data) could be structured as a value exchange. Contact point: SLS NSW Innovation team.

**Assessment:** Feasible via a triangulated partnership — approach DPIRD first, then propose SLS NSW integration as an output channel (e.g., SharkSense risk scores surfaced to patrol officers via a lightweight integration).

### 6.4.3 Dorsal App as a Template for Government Partnerships

**Dorsal's model:** Dorsal (dorsalwatch.com) aggregates official government reports, lifesaver sightings, fisheries data, and crowdsourced public reports. It explicitly states: "At Dorsal we aggregate as many official reports as possible from government bodies, lifesavers and fisheries. We are always looking for partners to share their data for the better interest of the public."

Dorsal operates as a community-based alert platform in four regions (Australia, Hawaii, Mainland USA, Reunion Island). In NSW, NSW Fisheries (DPIRD predecessor) was cited as an early data contributor. The model is a crowdsourced + official data hybrid, with volunteer verification of public reports.

**Template value for SharkSense:** Dorsal demonstrates that third-party apps can successfully aggregate official NSW government shark data without being the government. The precedent — NSW Fisheries feeding sighting data into a private app — validates the approach. However, Dorsal is primarily a sighting aggregator, not a risk predictor. SharkSense's predictive layer is genuinely differentiated.

**Key difference:** Dorsal's partnership is content (sighting reports). SharkSense's value proposition to government partners would be analytical (risk forecasting), which may be a harder sell but also a more defensible moat.

**Assessment:** Dorsal establishes that government data partnerships with private apps are achievable in the NSW shark management context. There is no evidence Dorsal made a formal "partnership offer to NSW Government" as a documented template — rather, the relationship appears to have developed informally. SharkSense should treat Dorsal as proof of concept rather than a replicable contractual template.

### 6.4.4 IMOS Animal Tracking Database

**Access model:** Open access via AODN portal (https://animaltracking.aodn.org.au/). Bulk download of historical detection data is available for most species. A small proportion of datasets (particularly threatened species) have embargoed or protected access requiring researcher application.

**What's available:** 67 million validated detections, 7,800 tags, 154 species, from 1,891 receiving stations Australia-wide. NSW station detections are included. This is the richest historical dataset for validating SharkSense's white shark (upwelling-driven) risk model.

**Restrictions:** Equipment loans from IMOS are restricted to marine areas (excludes estuaries and enclosed bays) — relevant for any future SharkSense-initiated tagging. Data access itself is broader.

**Assessment:** High feasibility. IMOS data is the most accessible and most useful historical dataset for SharkSense. Register as a user at animaltracking.aodn.org.au. For embargoed white shark data, contact Rob Harcourt at Macquarie University.

## Cross-Cutting Implications for SharkSense

1. **Data pipeline alignment:** The infrastructure transition to listening stations + drones (away from nets) plays directly to SharkSense's data architecture, which already includes IMOS animal tracking and @NSWSharkSmart scraping.

2. **Sydney Harbour bull shark focus:** The January 2026 tagging and harbour listening station program is directly relevant to three of SharkSense's five pilot beaches (Nielsen Park, Manly, Bondi approaches via harbour). Rainfall-driven bull shark risk scoring should improve as harbour detection data accumulates.

3. **Regulatory headroom:** No Australian law restricts publishing shark risk assessments. SharkSense is legally comparable to a weather forecast app. The ACL "due care and skill" guarantee means the algorithm must be defensible — poor weighting or clearly erroneous outputs create legal exposure regardless of disclaimers.

4. **Coexistence imperative:** All government partners, community groups, and media coverage frame shark management as coexistence, not eradication. SharkSense's messaging must align — risk scores should inform, not alarm.

5. **Parliamentary inquiry wildcard:** The potential NSW parliamentary committee inquiry into shark management (flagged March 2026) could result in policy changes affecting infrastructure deployment, data sharing obligations, or public communication requirements. Monitor progress.
